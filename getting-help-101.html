Getting Help 101

College can be overwhelming, but you don’t have to face it alone. At OSU, learning how to ask for help is a vital skill. Whether you're struggling with coursework, feeling lost, or just not sure where to go, this guide will walk you through how to:

  • Identify when you need help
  • Email a professor or advisor confidently
  • Speak up during office hours or class discussions
  • Find peer mentors or study groups

It’s okay to feel unsure. What matters is reaching out.

How do you know when you need help?

The answer to this question is simpler than it may sound. Ask yourself, do you find it overly stressful to reach any goal or perform any task with regards to college? Are you struggling with your grades? Is it overwhelming to find everything you need to sign up and prepare for a semester? Are you nervous about asking your professors for help? If the answer to any of these questions is yes, you probably need help.

Especially for first-generation students, I would recommend getting help. It is not at all an embarrassing matter to ask for help—everyone that succeeds in college gets help more often than you may think.

How do you ask a professor or advisor for help?

The most important part of asking for help is often professionality. Professionals like to see other professionals. The most common way of asking for help in our time is by emailing the professor or advisor you need help from.

A professional email is written in a specific format used by most that I will explain shortly. But before you start, keep in mind the conciseness of your email—professors and advisors run a tight schedule, you have to get your message through in the shortest possible time and as clear as possible. The format used is as follows: 

Dear [Professor/Dr Last Name] [Mr/Mrs/Ms. Last Name],

[Clear Line]
Message
[Clear Line]

[Thank you/salutations/sincerely, your Full Name]

How do you find and make use of office hours?

Office hours are very helpful for getting personal help from professors, as they know the most about how to succeed in their class. Professors typically list their office hours on their Carmen course page—check the syllabus, the home page, or the announcements section. If you can't find it, don’t hesitate to email them and ask directly.

Once you’ve found their office hours, show up with specific questions or concerns in mind. Be respectful of their time, and if other students are waiting, try to keep your visit focused. You can also email them in advance if you'd like to let them know what you'll be asking about. This shows initiative and helps them prepare to help you better.

How can peer mentors and study groups help?

Sometimes, the best help comes from people who’ve been in your shoes. Peer mentors and study groups offer a more informal, relatable way to get support. Many colleges within OSU have mentoring programs where upperclassmen guide newer students. Look out for announcements on your department's page or talk to an advisor to find a match.

Study groups are also incredibly effective—they allow you to compare notes, reinforce your understanding, and stay motivated. You can form one with classmates or use resources like the Dennis Learning Center or Younkin Success Center to find organized group sessions.

Remember, asking for help isn’t a weakness—it’s a strength. NSLB is here to make sure no students get left behind.
